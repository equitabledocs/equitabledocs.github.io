Skill building

What you will learn

The Guild is built around learning by doing. You work on real documents, receive structured training, and build practical skills in document accessibility — a field where skilled practitioners are urgently needed worldwide.

PDF remediation

Learn to tag PDFs for screen readers — headings, reading order, lists, figures, and metadata. Understand PDF/UA compliance and how to validate your work.

Word and PowerPoint accessibility

Create accessible documents from source — proper heading structures, alt text, table markup, and slide design that works with assistive technology.

Alt text and image descriptions

Write meaningful descriptions for images, charts, diagrams, and complex figures — the skill that most directly helps students using screen readers.

STEM and equation accessibility

Make mathematical equations, scientific notation, and data tables accessible using MathML and plain-language descriptions.

OCR and scanned documents

Learn how optical character recognition converts scanned pages into searchable text, and how to handle multi-language and complex layout documents.

Easy Read content creation

Simplify complex academic content into Easy Read format — clear language, short sentences, meaningful images — for students with learning and intellectual disabilities.

EPUB accessibility

Understand accessible digital publishing — semantic structure, navigation, media overlays, and how EPUB works with reading apps and assistive technology.

Quality assurance and validation

Learn to use industry-standard tools to validate documents against international accessibility standards, and how to do screen reader spot-tests.

Your contribution

How your work helps students

Every document you make accessible goes directly to a student who needs it. Your work supports schools, colleges, and universities in the Global South that cannot afford commercial document remediation services.

Real documents, real students

You work on actual academic documents — textbooks, reading lists, exam papers, course materials — submitted by students and institutions who need them made accessible.

Flexible hours, remote work

We ask for a few hours each week — but the schedule is yours. Work from anywhere, on any device, at any time that suits you. What matters is consistency, not clock hours.

Learn and share together

Community learning sessions, knowledge sharing, and the chance to co-author accessibility guides and best practice resources with fellow volunteers.

Volunteer roles

Four roles. A natural progression.

Everyone starts as a Learner. As you build skills and contribute, you grow naturally into roles with more responsibility. There are no exams, no scores — just consistent contribution and a willingness to learn and share.

Learner

You are new to document accessibility. You receive structured training, work on guided tasks, and learn the foundations alongside experienced volunteers. No prior experience needed.

Contributor

You work independently on document remediation tasks across your chosen focus areas. Your work directly supports students and institutions. You participate in community learning sessions and share what you have learned.

Trainer

You help train new volunteers and deliver accessibility training at partner universities. You contribute to training materials and accessibility guides. Certified Trainer credential from The Document Equity Initiative.

Mentor

You guide the Guild community — shaping standards, mentoring volunteers, and representing the initiative externally. You help decide which institutions the Guild supports next and how the community grows.

Common questions

Frequently asked questions

Do I need any experience to join?+
No. The Learner role is designed for people with no prior experience. You receive free training, mentorship, and guided tasks. All you need is a computer, a web browser, and the willingness to learn.
How much time do I need to commit?+
We ask volunteers to commit a few hours each week — but the schedule is entirely flexible. You choose when you work, from wherever you are. Some volunteers contribute an hour a day, others dedicate a weekend morning. What matters is consistency, not clock hours.
Is this paid work?+
The Document Accessibility Guild is volunteer-driven. You contribute your skills and time to help students with disabilities access their education. In return, you receive structured training, recognised certificates, and the opportunity to be part of a global community making a real difference. The value is genuine skill building, social impact, and being part of something meaningful.
What recognition will I receive?+
Every volunteer receives a certificate from The Document Equity Initiative documenting their contribution. You are listed on the EquitableDocs website as a Guild contributor. Trainers receive a Certified Trainer credential. Every tier documents your contribution and the impact your work has had on students.
What tools or software do I need?+
A computer and a web browser — that is all. The Guild works through a browser-based portal. No software to install. It works on any device — desktop, laptop, tablet, or phone.
Can I join from anywhere in the world?+
Yes. The Guild is open to anyone, anywhere. Volunteers who speak regional languages are especially valuable — our work covers documents in Hindi, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, and many other languages. All work is remote and browser-based.
